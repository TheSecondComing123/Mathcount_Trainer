Statistics

Statistics appears in about 10-15% of MathCounts problems, focusing on data analysis, measures of central tendency, and interpretation of graphs and charts.

Measures of Central Tendency

Mean (Average)

The sum of all values divided by the number of values.

Mean = (Sum of all values) / (Number of values)
Example 1: Easy
Find the mean of the data set: 12, 15, 18, 22, 28
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Sum all values

12 + 15 + 18 + 22 + 28 = 95

Step 2: Divide by number of values

Mean = 95 / 5 = 19

Answer: 19

Median

The middle value when data is arranged in order. If there's an even number of values, it's the average of the two middle values.

Example 2: Easy
Find the median of: 7, 3, 9, 12, 5, 8, 11
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Arrange in order

3, 5, 7, 8, 9, 11, 12

Step 2: Find middle value

7 values → middle is the 4th value

Median = 8

Answer: 8

Mode

The value that appears most frequently in the data set. There can be no mode, one mode, or multiple modes.

Example 3: Easy
Find the mode of: 4, 7, 9, 7, 12, 7, 15, 9
▶ Show Solution

Count frequency of each value:

4: appears 1 time

7: appears 3 times

9: appears 2 times

12: appears 1 time

15: appears 1 time

Answer: Mode = 7 (appears most frequently)

Measures of Spread

Range

The difference between the maximum and minimum values.

Range = Maximum value - Minimum value

Interquartile Range (IQR)

The range of the middle 50% of the data.

  • Q1 (First Quartile): Median of the lower half
  • Q2 (Second Quartile): Median of entire data set
  • Q3 (Third Quartile): Median of the upper half
  • IQR: Q3 - Q1
Example 4: Medium
Find the range and IQR of: 2, 5, 7, 9, 12, 15, 18, 22, 25
▶ Show Solution

Finding Range:

Range = 25 - 2 = 23

Finding IQR:

Step 1: Find Q2 (median of all 9 values)

Q2 = 12 (5th value)

Step 2: Find Q1 (median of lower half: 2, 5, 7, 9)

Q1 = (5 + 7)/2 = 6

Step 3: Find Q3 (median of upper half: 15, 18, 22, 25)

Q3 = (18 + 22)/2 = 20

Step 4: Calculate IQR

IQR = Q3 - Q1 = 20 - 6 = 14

Answer: Range = 23, IQR = 14

Working with Averages

Finding Missing Values

Example 5: Medium
The mean of four numbers is 20. Three of the numbers are 15, 18, and 22. What is the fourth number?
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Use the mean formula

Mean = (Sum of values) / 4

20 = (15 + 18 + 22 + x) / 4

Step 2: Solve for x

80 = 55 + x

x = 25

Answer: The fourth number is 25

Weighted Averages

When values have different weights or frequencies:

Weighted Mean = Σ(value × weight) / Σ(weights)
Example 6: Medium
A student's grade is determined by: Tests (60%), Homework (25%), and Final Exam (15%). If the test average is 85, homework average is 92, and final exam score is 88, what is the final grade?
▶ Show Solution

Apply weighted average formula:

Step 1: Multiply each component by its weight

Tests: 85 × 0.60 = 51

Homework: 92 × 0.25 = 23

Final: 88 × 0.15 = 13.2

Step 2: Sum the weighted values

Final Grade = 51 + 23 + 13.2 = 87.2

Answer: 87.2

Data Interpretation

Reading Tables and Charts

MathCounts frequently tests your ability to extract information from visual data representations.

Chart Reading Tips

  • Always read axis labels carefully
  • Check the scale (intervals between marks)
  • Look for units of measurement
  • Read titles and legends

Common Graph Types

  • Bar Graph: Compares different categories
  • Line Graph: Shows changes over time
  • Pie Chart: Shows parts of a whole (percentages)
  • Scatter Plot: Shows relationship between two variables
  • Histogram: Shows frequency distribution of data

Box-and-Whisker Plots

Box plots display the five-number summary of a data set:

  • Minimum
  • Q1 (First Quartile)
  • Q2 (Median)
  • Q3 (Third Quartile)
  • Maximum

Box Plot Components

  • Box: Extends from Q1 to Q3 (contains middle 50% of data)
  • Line in Box: Represents the median (Q2)
  • Whiskers: Extend to minimum and maximum values
  • Width of Box: IQR (Q3 - Q1)

Advanced Statistical Concepts

Outliers

Values that are significantly different from other values in the data set.

A common rule: A value is an outlier if:

  • It's less than Q1 - 1.5 × IQR, or
  • It's greater than Q3 + 1.5 × IQR

Mean vs Median

When to Use Mean

  • Data is relatively symmetric
  • No extreme outliers
  • Want to use all data values

When to Use Median

  • Data has outliers
  • Data is skewed
  • Want resistant measure

Sample Problems

Example 7: Hard
The mean of 10 numbers is 50. If one number (60) is removed, what is the mean of the remaining 9 numbers?
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Find sum of original 10 numbers

Mean = Sum / Count

50 = Sum / 10

Sum = 500

Step 2: Subtract the removed number

New sum = 500 - 60 = 440

Step 3: Calculate new mean

New mean = 440 / 9 ≈ 48.89

Answer: 440/9 or approximately 48.89

Example 8: Medium
The median of 5 consecutive integers is 12. What is their sum?
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Identify the 5 consecutive integers

With 5 numbers, median is the middle (3rd) value

The 5 integers are: 10, 11, 12, 13, 14

Step 2: Sum them

10 + 11 + 12 + 13 + 14 = 60

Shortcut: Sum = median × count = 12 × 5 = 60

Answer: 60

MathCounts-Specific Tips

Quick Strategies

  • Always arrange data in order first
  • Use sum formulas to work backwards
  • Check if data is consecutive/arithmetic
  • Look for patterns before calculating

Key Formulas

  • Mean = Sum / Count
  • Range = Max - Min
  • IQR = Q3 - Q1
  • Sum = Mean × Count

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting to order data before finding median
  • Confusing mean, median, and mode
  • Not averaging the two middle values (even count)
  • Misreading graph scales
