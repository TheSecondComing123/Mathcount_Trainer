Counting & Probability

Counting and probability problems appear in 15-20% of MathCounts questions. These topics test your logical thinking, systematic counting, and understanding of random events.

Fundamental Counting Principle

If event A can occur in m ways and event B can occur in n ways, then both events can occur in m × n ways.
Example 1: Easy
A restaurant offers 4 types of sandwiches and 3 types of drinks. How many different meal combinations are possible?
▶ Show Solution

Apply the Fundamental Counting Principle:

Step 1: Identify independent choices

Sandwiches: 4 choices

Drinks: 3 choices

Step 2: Multiply the number of choices

Total combinations = 4 × 3 = 12

Answer: 12 different meal combinations

Permutations

Permutations count arrangements where order matters.

Permutation Formula

P(n, r) = n!/(n-r)!

This represents the number of ways to arrange r objects from n total objects.

Factorial Notation

n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × 2 × 1

Special case: 0! = 1

Quick Factorials

  • 1! = 1
  • 2! = 2
  • 3! = 6
  • 4! = 24
  • 5! = 120
  • 6! = 720
  • 7! = 5,040
  • 8! = 40,320
Example 2: Medium
How many ways can 5 students be arranged in a row for a photo?
▶ Show Solution

This is a permutation problem (order matters):

Step 1: Recognize we're arranging all 5 students

P(5, 5) = 5!

Step 2: Calculate 5!

5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120

Answer: 120 different arrangements

Example 3: Medium
How many 3-digit numbers can be formed using the digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 without repetition?
▶ Show Solution

Choosing 3 digits from 5, order matters:

Step 1: Use permutation formula

P(5, 3) = 5!/(5-3)! = 5!/2!

Step 2: Calculate

P(5, 3) = (5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1)/(2 × 1)

= 120/2 = 60

Alternative: Counting Principle

First digit: 5 choices

Second digit: 4 choices

Third digit: 3 choices

Total = 5 × 4 × 3 = 60

Answer: 60 three-digit numbers

Combinations

Combinations count selections where order does NOT matter.

Combination Formula

C(n, r) = n!/[r!(n-r)!]

Also written as nCr or (n choose r).

Permutation vs Combination

  • Permutation: Order matters (arranging, ranking, sequencing)
  • Combination: Order doesn't matter (selecting, choosing, grouping)
Example 4: Medium
A pizza shop offers 8 toppings. How many different 3-topping pizzas can be made?
▶ Show Solution

This is a combination (order doesn't matter):

Step 1: Apply combination formula

C(8, 3) = 8!/[3!(8-3)!] = 8!/(3!5!)

Step 2: Simplify

C(8, 3) = (8 × 7 × 6)/(3 × 2 × 1)

= 336/6 = 56

Answer: 56 different pizzas

Useful Combination Properties

  • C(n, 0) = 1 (one way to choose nothing)
  • C(n, n) = 1 (one way to choose everything)
  • C(n, r) = C(n, n-r) (symmetry property)
  • C(n, 1) = n (n ways to choose 1 item)

Basic Probability

Probability Definition

P(event) = (Number of favorable outcomes)/(Total number of possible outcomes)

Probability Rules

  • Probability ranges from 0 to 1 (or 0% to 100%)
  • P(certain event) = 1
  • P(impossible event) = 0
  • P(not A) = 1 - P(A)
Example 5: Easy
What is the probability of rolling a number greater than 4 on a standard die?
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Identify favorable outcomes

Numbers greater than 4: {5, 6}

Favorable outcomes = 2

Step 2: Identify total outcomes

Total outcomes = 6 (numbers 1-6)

Step 3: Calculate probability

P(>4) = 2/6 = 1/3

Answer: 1/3 or approximately 33.3%

Compound Probability

Independent Events

Events are independent if the occurrence of one doesn't affect the other.

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
Example 6: Medium
What is the probability of flipping a coin twice and getting heads both times?
▶ Show Solution

These are independent events:

Step 1: Find probability of each event

P(heads on first flip) = 1/2

P(heads on second flip) = 1/2

Step 2: Multiply for independent events

P(both heads) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4

Answer: 1/4 or 25%

Mutually Exclusive Events

Events that cannot occur simultaneously.

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

General Addition Rule

For any two events:

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)
Example 7: Medium
A bag contains 3 red marbles, 4 blue marbles, and 5 green marbles. What is the probability of drawing a red or blue marble?
▶ Show Solution

These are mutually exclusive events:

Step 1: Find individual probabilities

Total marbles = 3 + 4 + 5 = 12

P(red) = 3/12 = 1/4

P(blue) = 4/12 = 1/3

Step 2: Add probabilities (mutually exclusive)

P(red or blue) = 1/4 + 1/3

= 3/12 + 4/12 = 7/12

Answer: 7/12

Dependent Events and Conditional Probability

Dependent Events

When the first event affects the probability of the second event:

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A)

where P(B|A) is the probability of B given that A has occurred.

Example 8: Hard
A box contains 5 red balls and 3 blue balls. If two balls are drawn without replacement, what is the probability that both are red?
▶ Show Solution

Without replacement makes these dependent events:

Step 1: Probability of first red ball

P(first red) = 5/8

Step 2: Probability of second red ball (given first was red)

After removing one red ball: 4 red, 3 blue remain

P(second red | first red) = 4/7

Step 3: Multiply probabilities

P(both red) = 5/8 × 4/7 = 20/56 = 5/14

Answer: 5/14

Expected Value

Expected value is the average outcome of an experiment if repeated many times.

E(X) = Σ [value × probability]
Example 9: Medium
A game costs $2 to play. You roll a die and win $5 if you roll a 6, otherwise you win nothing. What is the expected value of playing this game?
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Identify outcomes and probabilities

Win $5: probability = 1/6

Win $0: probability = 5/6

Step 2: Calculate expected winnings

E(winnings) = 5 × (1/6) + 0 × (5/6)

= 5/6 ≈ $0.83

Step 3: Subtract cost to play

Expected value = 5/6 - 2

= 5/6 - 12/6 = -7/6 ≈ -$1.17

Answer: Expected value is -$1.17 (you expect to lose about $1.17 per game)

Advanced Counting Techniques

Casework

Breaking a problem into non-overlapping cases and summing the results.

Complementary Counting

Sometimes it's easier to count what you DON'T want, then subtract from the total.

Count of desired outcomes = Total - Count of unwanted outcomes

Stars and Bars

A technique for counting ways to distribute identical objects into distinct groups.

Example 10: Hard
How many 4-digit numbers contain at least one 5?
▶ Show Solution

Using Complementary Counting:

Step 1: Count total 4-digit numbers

First digit: 1-9 (9 choices)

Other digits: 0-9 (10 choices each)

Total = 9 × 10 × 10 × 10 = 9000

Step 2: Count 4-digit numbers with NO 5

First digit: 1-4, 6-9 (8 choices)

Other digits: 0-4, 6-9 (9 choices each)

No 5's = 8 × 9 × 9 × 9 = 5832

Step 3: Subtract

At least one 5 = 9000 - 5832 = 3168

Answer: 3168 four-digit numbers

MathCounts-Specific Tips

Problem-Solving Strategies

  • Draw tree diagrams for complex problems
  • Use complementary counting when appropriate
  • Check if order matters (permutation vs combination)
  • Verify answer makes sense (can't exceed total outcomes)

Key Formulas to Memorize

  • P(n,r) = n!/(n-r)!
  • C(n,r) = n!/[r!(n-r)!]
  • P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) for independent events
  • P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) for mutually exclusive events

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing permutations and combinations
  • Forgetting to account for replacement/no replacement
  • Adding probabilities that aren't mutually exclusive
  • Miscounting total outcomes
