Geometry

Geometry comprises about 20-25% of MathCounts problems. Success requires knowing formulas, understanding geometric properties, and developing strong spatial reasoning skills.

Essential Geometric Formulas

2D Shapes - Area Formulas

Shape Area Formula Key Variables
Rectangle A = length × width l = length, w = width
Square A = s² s = side length
Triangle A = ½bh b = base, h = height
Trapezoid A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h b₁, b₂ = parallel bases, h = height
Circle A = πr² r = radius
Parallelogram A = base × height b = base, h = perpendicular height

2D Shapes - Perimeter/Circumference

Shape Perimeter Formula
Rectangle P = 2(l + w)
Square P = 4s
Triangle P = a + b + c
Circle C = 2πr = πd

3D Shapes - Volume and Surface Area

Shape Volume Surface Area
Cube V = s³ SA = 6s²
Rectangular Prism V = lwh SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)
Cylinder V = πr²h SA = 2πr² + 2πrh
Cone V = ⅓πr²h SA = πr² + πrl (l = slant height)
Sphere V = ⅔πr³ SA = 4πr²
Pyramid V = ⅓Bh SA = B + lateral area (B = base area)

Triangle Properties

Triangles are Critical!

Triangle problems appear frequently in MathCounts. Master these properties for quick solutions.

Triangle Classification

  • By Sides: Equilateral (3 equal), Isosceles (2 equal), Scalene (none equal)
  • By Angles: Acute (all < 90°), Right (one = 90°), Obtuse (one > 90°)

Important Triangle Theorems

1. Pythagorean Theorem

a² + b² = c² (for right triangles, where c is the hypotenuse)

Common Pythagorean Triples

Memorize these to save time:

  • 3-4-5 (and multiples: 6-8-10, 9-12-15, etc.)
  • 5-12-13 (and multiples: 10-24-26, etc.)
  • 8-15-17
  • 7-24-25
Example 1: Easy
A right triangle has legs of length 9 and 12. What is the length of the hypotenuse?
▶ Show Solution

Method 1: Recognize the Pythagorean Triple

9-12-? is a multiple of 3-4-5 (multiply by 3)

3 × 3 = 9, 4 × 3 = 12, 5 × 3 = 15

Answer: 15

Method 2: Use Pythagorean Theorem

a² + b² = c²

9² + 12² = c²

81 + 144 = c²

225 = c²

c = 15

2. Triangle Angle Sum

The sum of angles in any triangle = 180°

3. Triangle Inequality Theorem

The sum of any two sides must be greater than the third side.

4. Special Right Triangles

45-45-90 Triangle

Sides ratio: 1 : 1 : √2

If legs = x, then hypotenuse = x√2

30-60-90 Triangle

Sides ratio: 1 : √3 : 2

Short leg : Long leg : Hypotenuse

Example 2: Medium
In a 30-60-90 triangle, the shorter leg has length 5. What is the area of the triangle?
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Identify the sides using the 1 : √3 : 2 ratio

Short leg (opposite 30°) = 5

Long leg (opposite 60°) = 5√3

Hypotenuse = 10

Step 2: Calculate area using A = ½bh

The two legs are the base and height:

A = ½ × 5 × 5√3

A = (25√3)/2

Answer: (25√3)/2 square units

5. Similar Triangles

Triangles are similar if they have the same angles. Corresponding sides are proportional.

Example 3: Medium
Two similar triangles have corresponding sides in the ratio 2:3. If the smaller triangle has an area of 8 square units, what is the area of the larger triangle?
▶ Show Solution

Key Concept: When triangles are similar with side ratio a:b, their areas are in ratio a²:b²

Step 1: Find the area ratio

Side ratio = 2:3

Area ratio = 2²:3² = 4:9

Step 2: Set up proportion

4/9 = 8/x

4x = 72

x = 18

Answer: 18 square units

Circle Geometry

Circle Definitions

  • Radius (r): Distance from center to any point on the circle
  • Diameter (d): Distance across the circle through the center (d = 2r)
  • Chord: Line segment connecting two points on the circle
  • Arc: Part of the circumference
  • Sector: "Slice" of the circle (like a pizza slice)

Circle Formulas

Area = πr²
Circumference = 2πr = πd

Arc Length and Sector Area

For an arc with central angle θ (in degrees):

Arc Length = (θ/360) × 2πr
Sector Area = (θ/360) × πr²
Example 4: Medium
A circle has radius 6. What is the area of a sector with a 60° central angle?
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Use the sector area formula

Sector Area = (θ/360) × πr²

Step 2: Substitute values

Sector Area = (60/360) × π(6)²

Sector Area = (1/6) × 36π

Sector Area = 6π

Answer: 6π square units

Common Circle Mistakes

  • Confusing radius and diameter
  • Using diameter in the area formula (should be radius!)
  • Forgetting π in answers when required
  • Not simplifying π expressions

Coordinate Geometry

Distance Formula

Distance between points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]

Midpoint Formula

Midpoint of the segment connecting (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

M = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2)

Slope Formula

Slope of the line through (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁)
Example 5: Easy
What is the distance between points (2, 3) and (6, 6)?
▶ Show Solution

Step 1: Apply the distance formula

d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]

Step 2: Substitute values

d = √[(6 - 2)² + (6 - 3)²]

d = √[4² + 3²]

d = √[16 + 9]

d = √25

d = 5

Answer: 5 units

Advanced Geometry Concepts

Heron's Formula

Area of a triangle with sides a, b, c:

s = (a + b + c)/2 (semi-perimeter)
Area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]

Inscribed Angle Theorem

An inscribed angle is half the central angle that subtends the same arc.

Power of a Point

For two chords AB and CD intersecting at point P inside a circle:

AP × PB = CP × PD

MathCounts-Specific Geometry Tips

Quick Calculation Tips

  • Recognize Pythagorean triples immediately
  • Know special right triangle ratios by heart
  • Memorize common values: π ≈ 3.14, √2 ≈ 1.41, √3 ≈ 1.73
  • Draw diagrams even if not provided

Problem-Solving Strategies

  • Always draw and label a diagram
  • Look for similar or congruent triangles
  • Use auxiliary lines to create useful triangles
  • Check if units need conversion

Common Pitfalls

  • Using diameter instead of radius (or vice versa)
  • Mixing up perimeter and area
  • Forgetting to square/cube in volume problems
  • Incorrect unit conversions
